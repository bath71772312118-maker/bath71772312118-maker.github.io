02 Featured Project

Dexter — Robotic Arm Design, Failure Analysis & Redesign

A complete design–simulate–redesign cycle in Autodesk Fusion 360: a first-pass arm failed its safety target at the gripper, was root-caused, and rebuilt as a 4-DOF arm with cycloidal gear drives — then validated under static, modal, and buckling analysis.

Phase 1 — Not strong enough

Version 1: Initial Prototype

A simplified arm with a spur-gear gripper, evaluated under a 50 N gripper load plus gravity. The simulation flagged the gripper support as structurally inadequate.

1.585Min Safety Factor (target 2.0)

Phase 2 — Redesigned & validated

Dexter: 4-DOF Redesign

Fully articulated 4-DOF arm with cycloidal gear drives at every joint and a purpose-built gear-driven parallel gripper, validated against three independent simulation studies.

1.481Min Safety Factor (target 2.0)
158.9 MPaMax Von Mises (57.8% of yield)
213×Buckling Load Multiplier

Why Version 1 failed

The critical region was the junction between the forearm link and the spur-gear gripper support — a cantilevered point where all grasping forces concentrated as bending moments, with thin engagement zones causing high local contact pressure (25.97 MPa) and a base reaction force of 51.09 N from just a 50 N applied load. Rather than patch the frame, the arm was rebuilt from the ground up as Dexter: cycloidal gear drives for better load distribution, a wider gripper finger base, and an increased base wall thickness for moment resistance.

Dexter — simulation results

Three studies run on the final 4-DOF assembly, Aluminium Alloy (yield strength 275 MPa).

Static Stress — Safety Factor

1.481

Minimum at the lower arm joint, vs 8+ everywhere else on the assembly. Below the 2.0 target but stable for lightweight operation.

Von Mises Stress

158.882 MPa

Localised at the lower-arm joint — about 42% stress margin remaining against the 275 MPa yield strength of Aluminium Alloy.

Total Displacement

0.482 mm

At the gripper end-effector, on a ~320 mm reach arm — a tip deflection ratio of roughly 0.15%.

Modal Frequency — Mode 1

48.738 Hz

Well above the typical 1–20 Hz servo actuation range, confirming the arm avoids resonance during normal operation.

Structural Buckling

213×

Buckling load multiplier on Mode 1 — the arm can theoretically take 213× the applied compressive load before collapse.

End Effector — Full Hand v1

70 mm

Maximum gripping width on a spur-gear + linkage parallel gripper, designed for synchronised finger closure.

Outcome This project demonstrated a complete engineering design–simulate–redesign workflow: a baseline prototype was found structurally inadequate, root-caused to its gripper geometry, and rebuilt as a validated 4-DOF arm with documented static, modal, and buckling performance — forming a foundation for future motor integration, control system design, and physical prototyping.
